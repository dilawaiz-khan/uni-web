Computer Networks

Course Title: Computer Network

Course Code: Maj/SE-308
Credit Hours: 4 (3+1)
Prerequisites: None

Course Learning Outcomes (CLOs):

At the end of the course, students will be able to:

  1. CLO1: Describe key terminologies and technologies of computer networks.
    Domain: Cognitive
    BT Level: 2 (Understanding)
    PLO: Engineering Knowledge
  2. CLO2: Explain the services and functions provided by each layer in the Internet protocol stack.
    Domain: Cognitive
    BT Level: 2 (Understanding)
    PLO: Problem Analysis
  3. CLO3: Identify various internetworking devices and protocols and their functions in a networking environment.
    Domain: Cognitive
    BT Level: 4 (Analyzing)
    PLO: Design/Development of Solutions
  4. CLO4: Analyze the working and performance of key technologies, algorithms, and protocols.
    Domain: Cognitive
    BT Level: 4 (Analyzing)
    PLO: Design/Development of Solutions
  5. CLO5: Build and configure computer networks using various topologies and protocols.
    Domain: Psychomotor
    BT Level: 6 (Creating)
    PLO: Individual and Team Work

Program Learning Objectives (PLOs):

  1. PLO1: Engineering Knowledge: Apply knowledge of mathematics, science, engineering fundamentals, and an engineering specialization to solve complex engineering problems.
  2. PLO2: Problem Analysis: Identify, formulate, research literature, and analyze complex engineering problems reaching substantiated conclusions using first principles of mathematics, natural sciences, and engineering sciences.
  3. PLO3: Design/Development of Solutions: Design solutions for complex engineering problems and design systems, components, or processes that meet specified needs with appropriate consideration for public health and safety, cultural, societal, and environmental considerations.
  4. PLO4: Individual and Team Work: Function effectively as an individual, and as a member or leader in diverse teams and in multi-disciplinary settings.

Course Contents and Weekly Breakdown:

Week 1: Introduction to Computer Networks

  • Overview of computer networks, communication models, and network hardware
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 1: “Introduction to Computer Networks” from Computer Networks by Andrew S. Tanenbaum
  • Assignment: Write a summary of the different types of networks (LAN, MAN, WAN).
  • Lab Work: Introduction to network cables (Coaxial, Twisted Pair, Fiber-Optic). Practice creating and testing a basic straight-through cable.
  • Lab Assignment: Create and test a basic straight-through Ethernet cable.

Week 2: Protocol Hierarchies and Architecture

  • Overview of protocol hierarchies and architecture including the OSI and TCP/IP models
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 2: “Protocol Hierarchies” from Computer Networking: A Top-Down Approach by James F. Kurose and Keith W. Ross
  • Assignment: Create a comparative chart of the OSI and TCP/IP models.
  • Lab Work: Use a network simulator (Packet Tracer) to model and visualize the OSI and TCP/IP layers.
  • Lab Assignment: Build and configure a simple network in Packet Tracer, mapping out the OSI and TCP/IP models.

Week 3: Physical Layer and Media

  • Guided media: coaxial cable, twisted pair, fiber-optic cable, and transmission impairments
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 3: “The Physical Layer” from Data and Computer Communications by William Stallings
  • Assignment: Analyze the advantages and disadvantages of different guided media.
  • Lab Work: Experiment with different guided media and measure transmission characteristics.
  • Lab Assignment: Measure and compare the performance of coaxial, twisted pair, and fiber-optic cables.

Week 4: Network Topologies and Transmission Modes

  • Network topologies, analog and digital transmission modes, asynchronous and synchronous transmission
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 4: “Network Topologies and Transmission Modes” from Data Communication and Computer Networks by Behrouz A. Forouzan
  • Assignment: Design a network topology for a small office and explain your choices.
  • Lab Work: Create and test different network topologies using Packet Tracer.
  • Lab Assignment: Implement and test various network topologies (e.g., star, ring, mesh) in Packet Tracer.

Week 5: Multiplexing Techniques

  • Introduction to FDM, TDM, and WDM multiplexing
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 5: “Multiplexing” from Computer Networks by Andrew S. Tanenbaum
  • Assignment: Create a diagram showing how different multiplexing techniques are used.
  • Lab Work: Configure and test multiplexing techniques using network simulation tools.
  • Lab Assignment: Simulate and analyze FDM, TDM, and WDM techniques in Packet Tracer.

Week 6: Data Link Layer Protocols

  • Data link protocols: HDLC, PPP, error control, flow control
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 6: “Data Link Layer Protocols” from Computer Networking: A Top-Down Approach
  • Assignment: Implement a simulation of error detection and correction mechanisms.
  • Lab Work: Configure and test HDLC and PPP protocols in Packet Tracer.
  • Lab Assignment: Configure and analyze HDLC and PPP protocols in Packet Tracer, focusing on error control and flow control.

Week 7: Medium Access Control (MAC) and Switching

  • MAC sublayer, multiple access protocols (Ethernet: CSMA/CD, Token passing)
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 7: “Medium Access Control” from Data and Computer Communications
  • Assignment: Configure a network with Ethernet and demonstrate MAC addressing.
  • Lab Work: Set up a network with various MAC protocols and analyze performance.
  • Lab Assignment: Implement CSMA/CD and Token Passing protocols in Packet Tracer and analyze their impact on network performance.

Week 8: Mid-Term Review and Exam

  • Review of material covered in Weeks 1-7
  • Mid-Term Exam
  • Assignment: Compile a portfolio of all assignments and practical tasks completed so far.
  • Lab Work: Review and practice lab exercises from previous weeks.
  • Lab Assignment: Revise and practice key lab tasks covered in the first half of the course.

Week 9: Network Layer Functionality and Routing

  • Network layer functionality, routing algorithms (Link State, Distance Vector)
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 8: “Network Layer Functionality” from Data Communication and Computer Networks
  • Assignment: Analyze routing algorithms and create a routing table for a given network scenario.
  • Lab Work: Configure routing protocols (e.g., RIP) in Packet Tracer and analyze routing tables.
  • Lab Assignment: Set up and test routing protocols (RIP, OSPF) in Packet Tracer.

Week 10: Internetworking and Internet Addressing

  • Internetworking, IP addressing, subnetting, CIDR
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 9: “Internetworking and Addressing” from Computer Networks
  • Assignment: Create and configure subnets for a given IP address range.
  • Lab Work: Set up IP addressing and subnetting in Packet Tracer.
  • Lab Assignment: Configure IP addressing, subnetting, and CIDR in Packet Tracer.

Week 11: Switching Techniques and Congestion Control

  • Switching techniques, congestion control, detection, and recovery
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 10: “Switching Techniques and Congestion Control” from Computer Networking: A Top-Down Approach
  • Assignment: Simulate switching techniques and analyze congestion control methods.
  • Lab Work: Configure switching techniques and analyze network performance under different congestion scenarios.
  • Lab Assignment: Implement and troubleshoot various switching techniques and congestion control measures in Packet Tracer.

Week 12: Transport Layer Protocols

  • TCP and UDP, frame formats, session and presentation layers
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 11: “Transport Layer Protocols” from Effective Technical Communication by Ashraf Rizvi
  • Assignment: Compare TCP and UDP protocols and create a configuration for each.
  • Lab Work: Configure TCP and UDP protocols in Packet Tracer and analyze their performance.
  • Lab Assignment: Set up and test TCP and UDP connections, and analyze their differences.

Week 13: Network Security Basics

  • Goals, concerns, attacks, services, and mechanisms; firewalls, packet filtering
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 12: “Network Security Basics” from Computer Networking: A Top-Down Approach
  • Assignment: Design a basic firewall configuration and explain its purpose.
  • Lab Work: Implement and test firewall configurations in a simulated environment.
  • Lab Assignment: Configure and test firewall rules and settings in Packet Tracer.

Week 14: Firewall Configuration and Types

  • Different types of firewalls (software, hardware), characteristics, limitations
  • Reading Material:
    • Chapter 13: “Firewall Configuration” from Data and Computer Communications
  • Assignment: Compare different types of firewalls and their effectiveness.
  • Lab Work: Set up and configure various types of firewalls.
  • Lab Assignment: Implement and compare different firewall configurations in Packet Tracer.

Week 15: Project Work and Review

  • Preparation for final project presentations and review of key concepts
  • Assignment: Complete and finalize the semester project.
  • Lab Work: Review and practice lab exercises in preparation for the final project.
  • Lab Assignment: Finalize and test the semester project network configurations.

Week 16: Final Project Presentations and Review

  • Presentation of semester projects and review of course material
  • Assignment: Prepare and deliver a presentation of the semester project.
  • Lab Work: Present final projects and conduct peer reviews.
  • Lab Assignment: Submit the final project report and deliverables.

Grading Distribution:

  • Quizzes (10%): Surprise quizzes on course material to assess understanding.
  • Assignments (10%): Weekly assignments based on reading material and lab work.
  • Mid-Term Exam (30%): Covering the first half of the course.
  • Final Exam (30%): Comprehensive exam covering the entire course.
  • Lab Work (10%): Hands-on practice and lab exercises.
  • Semester Project (10%): Practical project demonstrating network design and configuration.
  • Class Participation (10%): Engagement in class discussions, activities, and presentations.
